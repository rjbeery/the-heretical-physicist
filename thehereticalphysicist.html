Core Idea

Black holes as “frozen stars,” not traversable horizons

Einstein argued that event horizons cannot form in finite time. I revisit that objection using explicit causal reasoning: infalling matter and light become causally frozen at the boundary, never crossing it. The result is operationally a zero‑way surface, not a permeable one-way street. My recent publications develop this argument at two levels: a lay-accessible thought experiment and a technical analysis with horizon‑chasing in dynamical spacetimes.

This work challenges the conventional view of black holes by demonstrating that the formation of event horizons faces fundamental causal limitations. Using both classical general relativity and careful consideration of causal structure, I show that the traditional "one-way membrane" picture of event horizons requires reassessment.

The implications are profound: rather than being gateways to exotic physics, black holes may be better understood as eternally collapsing objects or "frozen stars" — a view that resolves several theoretical paradoxes while remaining consistent with observational evidence.

The Heretical Physicist: Revisiting Einstein’s Objection to the Event Horizon

World Scientific, Reports in Advances of Physical Sciences, Open Access.

A concise thought experiment (inelastic rope) showing that causal contact with an infalling body never returns once it approaches the horizon — establishing the horizon as a zero‑way surface.

No Passing Zone (Redux): Horizon Chasing in Ingoing Vaidya Coordinates

World Scientific, Reports in Advances of Physical Sciences, Open Access.
